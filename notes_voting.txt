RCV - Chat Gpt
=========
You're referring to **Ranked Choice Voting (RCV)**, also known in some places as **Preferential Voting** or, depending on the counting method, the **Alternative Vote (AV)**.

Here's how it works.

## Step 1: Voters rank the candidates

Instead of choosing just one candidate, each voter ranks them in order of preference.

For example:

| Rank | Candidate |
| ---- | --------- |
| 1    | Alice     |
| 2    | Bob       |
| 3    | Carol     |
| 4    | David     |

The voter is saying:

> "I'd most like Alice to win. If Alice can't win, I'd prefer Bob. If Bob can't win, then Carol..."

## Step 2: Count first-choice votes

Suppose there are 100 voters.

| Candidate | 1st-choice votes |
| --------- | ---------------: |
| Alice     |               42 |
| Bob       |               31 |
| Carol     |               18 |
| David     |                9 |

To win, a candidate needs **more than 50%** of the active votes.

Nobody has that yet.

## Step 3: Eliminate the last-place candidate

David has the fewest votes (9), so he's eliminated.

Now look at those 9 ballots.

Perhaps they ranked:

* Bob second on 5 ballots
* Carol second on 3 ballots
* Alice second on 1 ballot

Those ballots are transferred.

Now the totals become:

| Candidate | Votes |
| --------- | ----: |
| Alice     |    43 |
| Bob       |    36 |
| Carol     |    21 |

Still no majority.

## Step 4: Eliminate the next-lowest

Carol is now last.

Transfer Carol's ballots according to the next available preference.

Suppose:

* 15 go to Bob
* 5 go to Alice
* 1 has no further preferences

Now:

| Candidate | Votes |
| --------- | ----: |
| Alice     |    48 |
| Bob       |    51 |

Bob now has a majority of the remaining active votes and wins.

---

## Why people like it

Instead of this:

```
Alice 42%
Bob   31%
Carol 18%
David  9%
```

Alice would win under a simple plurality system, even though **58% preferred someone else**.

With Ranked Choice Voting, voters can express backup preferences, which often produces a winner with broader overall support.

## Advantages

* Less "vote splitting."
* Fewer "wasted votes."
* Encourages more civil campaigning, since candidates benefit from being acceptable as a second or third choice.
* Can eliminate the need for a separate runoff election.

## Disadvantages

* More complicated to explain and count than "pick one."
* Ballots can become "exhausted" if voters don't rank enough candidates. For example, if all the candidates a voter ranked are eliminated and they didn't rank anyone else, their ballot no longer counts in later rounds.
* Some people find the ranking process confusing, especially when there are many candidates.

## Example ballot

```
□ Alice    (1)
□ Bob      (2)
□ Carol    (3)
□ David    (4)
```

The numbers indicate the voter's preference order.

## Different names

Depending on the country or context, you may hear:

* **Ranked Choice Voting (RCV)** – the most common term in the United States.
* **Preferential Voting** – common in Australia and elsewhere.
* **Alternative Vote (AV)** – specifically refers to the instant-runoff method used for single-winner elections.
* **Instant Runoff Voting (IRV)** – another name for the same single-winner counting method.
